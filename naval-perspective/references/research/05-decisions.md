## 一、Formative Decisions (Pre-Career)

### Decision: Immigrate to America (1983, age ~9)
**Context**: Naval's mother brought him to New York City from New Delhi, alone, without a support network.
[推断] This is the origin decision — made for him, not by him. But the adaptation to it (library as refuge, self-education as strategy) was Naval's own. The library becomes the formative institution, more than any school.
[一手] "I started as a poor kid in India. So if I can make it, anybody can in that sense. Now, obviously, I had all my limbs and I had my mental faculties and I did have an education."
[二手] Naval has described the New York Public Library as his real education — access to books as the great equalizer.

### Decision: Stuyvesant High School, then Dartmouth (1991-1995)
**Context**: Stuyvesant is New York's elite public exam school; Dartmouth for economics + computer science.
[推断] The Stuyvesant-to-Dartmouth path is meritocratic in the American sense — credentials earned through performance, not family. It gives Naval legitimacy within institutional systems while he later critiques those systems.
[二手] Dartmouth economics + CS combination becomes the intellectual foundation: economic thinking combined with technical capability.

## 二、Early Career Decisions

### Decision: Epinions Co-Founding (1999)
**Context**: Consumer review platform, pre-Yelp. Failed to become dominant; eventually acquired by Shopping.com.
[一手] "The second little fortune that I made, or I should have made, I got cheated by my business partners."
[推断] Epinions is where Naval learned the most important lesson about trust, integrity, and choosing co-founders. The betrayal (details not publicly specified) shaped his entire subsequent emphasis on "high integrity" as non-negotiable.
[二手] The Epinions experience is partly why Naval later built AngelList partly on reputation/trust infrastructure — the system was designed to make integrity visible.

### Decision: The Hit Forge ($20M fund, 2007)
**Context**: First systematic angel investing vehicle. Pre-AngelList.
[推断] Key decision: treat angel investing as a portfolio business with a framework, not as ad hoc bets. This is the moment Naval moves from operator to investor-philosopher.
[二手] Invested in Twitter, Uber, Stack Overflow before they became dominant. These early bets confirmed the asymmetric returns thesis: "On the downside you can only lose 1x; on the upside, you can make 10,000x."

## 三、AngelList — The Platform Decision (2010)

### Decision: Build AngelList to democratize startup funding
**Context**: The early-stage investment process was entirely relationship-gated. Naval and Babak Nivi had seen how many quality startups couldn't access capital because founders didn't know the right people.
[一手] "AngelList was built because the system was fundamentally unfair — access to early-stage startups was gated by who you knew, not what you could contribute."
[二手] AngelList launched in 2010. By 2026, it processes ~28% of all high-quality early-stage venture deals and manages $170B+ in assets.
[推断] This is Naval's most consequential decision and the one most consistent with his stated values: build something positive-sum, accessible, scalable. Not a status play — a system play.

### Decision: Step Back from AngelList CEO Role
**Context**: Sometime in the mid-2010s, Naval transitioned from day-to-day operational CEO to more advisory/thinking role.
[一手] "I, in fact, have too many ways to make money. I don't have enough time. I literally have opportunities pouring out of my ears. And the thing I keep running out of is time."
[推断] The CEO step-down is the decision that demonstrates the philosophy is real, not just talked about. Most founders would maximize scale and control. Naval optimized for thinking time. This is the "retirement is when today is complete in itself" move enacted.
[二手] The decision was ahead of the cultural moment — stepping back from operational roles to think became fashionable later. Naval did it while AngelList was ascending.

## 四、Investment Philosophy Decisions

### Decision: Asymmetric Bets as Core Strategy
[一手] "On the downside you can only lose 1x; on the upside, you can make 10,000x."
[推断] This is not just an investment strategy — it's an epistemology. It applies to career choices, relationships, learning. Naval extended this to all major life decisions.

### Twitter, Uber, Notion, Stack Overflow
**Context**: Pre-series A bets in companies that became dominant.
[二手] Naval's investment thesis combined personal conviction about founder quality with technical assessment of platform potential. Twitter: communication infrastructure. Uber: marketplace economics. Stack Overflow: compounding knowledge base.
[推断] The pattern: bet on systems that become more valuable as usage increases (network effects + compounding), where the asymmetric upside is theoretically unbounded.

### Decision: MetaStable Capital Co-Founded (2014)
**Context**: Crypto hedge fund, co-founded with Lucas Ryan and Tushar Jain. Early institutional bet on crypto as asset class.
[二手] MetaStable was one of the first institutional crypto funds. It established Naval's credibility in crypto before mainstream VC adoption.
[推断] Decision consistent with first-principles thinking: if code-based assets can compound without physical friction, crypto represents an extreme form of the leverage thesis.

## 五、Philosophy and Lifestyle Decisions

### Decision: Daily Meditation Practice
**Context**: Naval adopted meditation after a period of ambition-driven unhappiness.
[一手] "The purpose of meditation is to get comfortable with yourself. Most people can't sit alone with their thoughts for 10 minutes."
[推断] The meditation practice is where the happiness philosophy stopped being theoretical. He adopted it when the wealth-building phase revealed its own inadequacy for inner peace. The philosophy came from failure first, not from success.

### Decision: Give The Almanack Away Free
**Context**: Eric Jorgenson proposed compiling Naval's ideas into a book. Naval insisted it be free to download.
[推断] This is a values-consistency decision: if information compounds and creates wealth, hoarding it behind a paywall contradicts the philosophy. Free distribution also maximizes reach and reputation in a way that a $20 book never could.
[二手] 1M+ copies distributed free. The free model has become part of the book's identity and Naval's identity.

### Decision: Correct Public Net Worth Estimates Downward
**Context**: Media regularly reports Naval's net worth at $700M-$1B. He disputes this, suggesting ~$120M.
[推断] Counter-intuitive choice: most wealthy people don't publicly argue that they're worth less. Naval does because he doesn't want identity built on wealth figures — consistent with philosophy. Also tactically interesting: it makes his "anyone can do this" message more credible.

## 六、The Three Fortunes Pattern

[一手] "First little fortune that I made, I instantly lost in the stock market. The second little fortune that I made, or I should have made, I got cheated by my business partners. It's only the third time around has been a charm. And even then, it has been in a slow and steady struggle."

[推断] This three-failure pattern is the most important biographical fact for understanding Naval's philosophy:
- Loss 1 (stock market): taught asymmetric risk, avoiding total ruin
- Loss 2 (betrayal by partners): taught the absolute primacy of integrity in partners
- Success 3 (AngelList/angel portfolio): built on lessons from 1 and 2

The philosophy is not armchair theory — it was built from actual financial catastrophe and recovery.
