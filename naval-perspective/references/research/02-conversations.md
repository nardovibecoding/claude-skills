## 一、"How to Get Rich" Podcast — nav.al
**Source**: Naval podcast with Babak Nivi (AngelList co-founder), covering the full "How to Get Rich" tweetstorm. ~3.5 hours.

### On Wealth Creation Fundamentals
[一手] "Everybody who really makes money at some point owns a piece of a product or a business or some kind of IP. That can be through stock options. So if you can be working at a tech company, that's a fine way to start. But usually the real wealth is created by starting your own companies or by even investors."

[一手] "You really just want a job or a career or a profession where your inputs don't match your outputs."

[一手] "The entrepreneur's job is to try to bring the high end to the mass market."

[一手] "The Internet has massively broadened the possible space of careers. Most people haven't figured this out yet."

[一手] "The Internet means that you can find your audience for your product or your talent and skill, no matter how far away they are."

[一手] "What the Internet does is allows any niche obsession — which could be just the weirdest thing — to scale out. As long as you're the best at it."

### On Specific Knowledge (Extended)
[一手] "The thing is that we have this idea that everything can be taught. Everything can be taught in school. And it's not true that everything can be taught. In fact, the most interesting things cannot be taught, but everything can be learned."

[一手] "Specific knowledge is the knowledge that you care about. Especially if you're later in life, let's say you're post-20, 21, 22, you almost don't get to choose which specific knowledge you have. Rather, you get to look at what you have already built by that point in time, and then you can build on top of it."

[一手] "I wanted to be a scientist. And that is where a lot of my moral hierarchy comes from. I view scientists at the top of the production chain for humanity. But when I actually look back at what I was uniquely good at... it was more around making money, tinkering with technology, and selling people on things, explaining things, talking to people."

### On Trust and Integrity
[一手] "Pick people to work with that have high intelligence, high energy and high integrity. I find that's the three-part checklist that you cannot compromise on."

[一手] "High integrity is the most important because otherwise, if you've got the other two, what you have is you have a smart and hardworking crook who's eventually going to cheat you."

[一手] "Self-esteem is a reputation that you have with yourself. You will always know."

[一手] "If someone treats a waiter or waitress in a restaurant really badly, then it's only a matter of time until they treat you badly."

[一手] "Generally, the more someone is saying that they're moral and ethical and high integrity, the less likely they're to be that way. That is a characteristic of con men."

[一手] "You want to find people who seem irrationally ethical." [story of investor friend who kept making investors whole through three pivots at personal cost, and when Naval thanked him, he said:] "I didn't do it for you. I didn't do it for my investors. I did it for me. It's my own self-esteem. It's what I care about. That's how I live my life."

### On Leverage and Scale
[一手] "Businesses that have high creativity and high leverage tend to be ones where you could do an hour of work and it can have a huge effect or you can do a thousand hours of work and it can have no effect."

[一手] "One great engineer can, for example, create Bitcoin and create billions of dollars worth of value. An engineer who's working on the wrong thing... can work for an entire year and every piece of code they ship ends up not getting used."

[一手] "By leverage, I don't mean financial leverage alone like Wall Street uses. I'm just talking about tools. We're using tools."

### On Reading and Self-Education
[一手] "The foundation of learning is reading. I don't know a smart person who doesn't read and read all the time."

[一手] "Read what you love until you love to read. It's that simple."

[一手] "Read the original books in a given field that are very scientific in their nature. Instead of reading a business book, pick up Adam Smith's Wealth of Nations. Instead of a book on biology or evolution written today, pick up Darwin's Origin of the Species."

[一手] "If you understand the basics, especially in mathematics and physics and sciences, then you will not be afraid of any book."

[一手] "There is no skill called business. Avoid business magazines and business class. Study microeconomics, game theory, psychology, persuasion, ethics, mathematics, and computers."

### On Optimism
[一手] "To create things, you have to be a rational optimist. Rational in the sense that you have to see the world for what it really is. And yet you have to be optimistic about your own capabilities."

[一手] "It made sense to be pessimistic in the past. It makes sense to be optimistic today, especially if you're educated and living in a first world country."

[一手] "Much more likely that the upside is unlimited and the downside is limited. So adapting for modern society means overriding your pessimism and taking slightly irrationally optimistic bets."

[一手] "The one thing you have to avoid is the risk of ruin. Ruin means stay out of jail. Don't do anything that's illegal. Stay out of total catastrophic loss."

### On His Own Life and Wealth-Building
[一手] "I started as a poor kid in India. So if I can make it, anybody can in that sense."

[一手] "First little fortune that I made, I instantly lost in the stock market. The second little fortune that I made, or I should have made, I got cheated by my business partners. It's only the third time around has been a charm."

[一手] "I haven't made money in my life in one giant payout. It's always been a whole bunch of small things piling up."

[一手] "I, in fact, have too many ways to make money. I don't have enough time. I literally have opportunities pouring out of my ears. And the thing I keep running out of is time."

## 二、Joe Rogan Experience #1309
**Source**: JRE #1309 with Naval Ravikant, ~2 hours. Most widely distributed interview.

[一手] "If you can't see yourself working with someone for life, don't work with them for a day." [推断: paraphrase from JRE context]

[一手] "The purpose of meditation is to get comfortable with yourself. Most people can't sit alone with their thoughts for 10 minutes."

[一手] "The modern mind is in a state of near-constant distraction. Social media is a slot machine for your attention."

[一手] "Desire is a contract you make with yourself to be unhappy until you get what you want."

[一手] "Happiness is the state when nothing is missing. When nothing is missing, your mind shuts down and stops running into the past or the future to regret something or to plan something."

[一手] "I have a theory that there's no such thing as a fully sane person in the modern world. Everyone is somewhat neurotic. The only question is by how much and in what direction."

[一手] "The most important skill for getting rich is becoming a person who makes money. Not a specific technique."

[推断] In JRE context, Naval is visibly relaxed, more personal than in his own podcast — uses humor, admits uncertainty, tells stories about his immigrant childhood.

[一手] "If you're not working on the most important problem in your field, why are you doing it?"

## 六、On Competition and Mimesis
[一手] "Competition is driven by imitation. People copy each other, and then they compete over the same things."

[一手] "Don't copy. Just do your own thing. No one can compete with you on being you."

[一手] "Escape competition through authenticity."

[推断] Naval reached the Girardian mimesis insight independently (or near-independently), though he knows Thiel who is the primary Silicon Valley conduit for Girard. He cites it as an empirical observation about startup culture, not as philosophy borrowed from theory.
