# Richard Branson — Chronological Milestones

> Research Date: 2026-04-07
> Tags: [一手] = Branson's own account | [二手] = External record | [推断] = Inferred significance

---

## 一、Early Life & Formation (1950–1969)

**1950** — Born July 18, Blackheath, London. Full name: Sir Richard Charles Nicholas Branson.
[二手] Upper-middle-class English family. Father Ted Branson, barrister. Mother Eve Branson, former actress/air hostess — the entrepreneurial influence.

**1954 (age ~4)** — Mother pushes him out of the car a mile from his grandparents' house.
[一手] "She was the kind of mother that felt we needed to stand on our own two feet. I got lost in the fields and ended up being rescued by a farmer."
[推断] Founding mythology of self-reliance. He retells this story often — it's where he locates the beginning of his independent streak.

**~1957 (age ~7)** — Sent to boarding school.
[一手] "I was sent away to boarding school at seven and a half. You were called into the headmaster's study in your pajamas. You had to take the pajama bottoms down and you were often beaten until you bled."
[推断] The boarding school experience — dyslexia undiagnosed, branded "thick," physical punishment — created two things: the outsider identity that fueled his challenger instinct, and an early ability to endure institutional hostility.

**~1961–1966** — Stowe School. Dyslexia undiagnosed. Terrible academic performance.
[一手] "People just thought I was thick or stupid at school. I got beaten regularly."
[二手] Headmaster's famous observation on departure: "You will either go to prison or become a millionaire."

**1966 (age 15–16)** — Founds Student magazine from school phone box. Interviews John Lennon.
[一手] "I was working out of the school phone box. I learned the art of bull quite early on — if you said to Coke that Pepsi were interested in taking an ad, they would jump in."
[一手] Gets 50,000 copies printed by selling advertising. Headmaster gives him the ultimatum: school or magazine.
[一手] "Just turning 16 and I'm off. Thank you very much."

## 二、Building Virgin Records (1970–1983)

**1970** — Student magazine transitions toward mail order records. Virgin name chosen.
[二手] The name "Virgin" came from being "virgins in business" — new, untested. Registration authority initially refused the name as rude.
[一手] "I got the English dictionary out. It said: pure, untouched, unblemished, perfection. I wrote to them and said this is the opposite of rude. They finally caved in."

**1971** — First Virgin Records shop opens on Oxford Street, London.
[二手] The shop's chill-out culture — headphones, bean bags — was itself a challenger move against stiff traditional record shops.

**1972** — Virgin Records record label founded. First signing: Mike Oldfield.

**1973** — "Tubular Bells" released. Sells 5 million copies worldwide. Virgin Records established as a serious label.
[推断] This was the first real proof of Branson's talent-spotting instinct — Oldfield had been rejected by every major label. The pattern of "sign what others reject" begins here.

**1977** — Signs the Sex Pistols after every other label dropped them post-Bill Grundy TV incident.
[二手] Commercially chaotic. Branson gets death threats. But PR value enormous — Virgin Records becomes the label for music the establishment fears.
[推断] First major demonstration that controversy is a branding opportunity, not a risk to avoid.

**Early 1980s** — Virgin Records grows. Signs: Culture Club, Genesis, Phil Collins, Janet Jackson.
[一手] "By then Virgin Records was the most successful independent record label in the world."

**~1982–1983** — Rolling Stones bid. Branson flies Germany/France/Italy in a weekend to raise £6 million.
[一手] "On Monday I rang him up and said, we have your 6 million. What he did was go to EMI and get them to increase their 3 million to 6 million." Outbid. Rolling Stones eventually signed with Virgin years later.

## 三、Virgin Atlantic & The Airline Wars (1984–1993)

**1984 (June)** — Virgin Atlantic launches.
[一手] "I was in Puerto Rico and I'd been bumped by American Airlines. I was determined to get to see my girlfriend. I went to the back of the airport, borrowed a blackboard, hired a plane, wrote as a joke: 'Virgin Airlines, one way, $39 to the BVI.' I filled up my first plane."
[一手] "I negotiated with Boeing so that if the airline didn't work in year one, they'd take the plane back." — The "protect the downside" principle in its purest form.
[二手] British Airways CEO Lord King's response: "Too young to fly, too old to rock and roll. They're never going to succeed."

**~1984–1991** — British Airways dirty tricks campaign.
[一手] "British Airways launched what became famously known as the British Airways dirty tricks campaign. It was a kneecapping job to try to put Virgin Atlantic out of business completely."
[一手] "They had teams ringing our passengers pretending to be from Virgin. They intercepted passengers at the airport. They went through my rubbish bins. They went through journalists' rubbish bins."

**1986** — Virgin Atlantic speed record attempt on the Atlantic. Refueling disaster.
[一手] "Half the fuel was water. Margaret Thatcher scrambled a Nimrod, which parachuted filters to us. On the first occasion, we sank. On the second occasion, we were successful and brought the record back to Britain."

**1991** — Virgin Records sale to EMI for $1 billion.
[二手] Branson reportedly cried in the street after signing.
[一手] "It gave us the financial muscle to not only combat British Airways but to move Virgin into whole massive new areas. Virgin became a sort of way of life brand."
[推断] His most painful decision. Also his most structurally important: the sale proceeds funded the next 20 years of Virgin diversification.

**1992–1993** — Virgin sues British Airways for libel. Wins largest libel damages in British history.
[一手] "We distributed them to all our staff equally. It became known as the 'British Airways Christmas bonus.'"
[二手] Lord King steps down from British Airways following the verdict.

## 四、Adventures & Records (1985–2000)

**1985** — Breaks the Atlantic Ocean speed record by boat (Blue Riband / Hales Trophy).

**1986** — Atlantic crossing by boat fails first attempt (sank, rescued by banana boat). Succeeds second attempt.

**1987** — First Atlantic crossing by hot air balloon (with Per Lindstrand).
[一手] "Per threw himself out of the capsule into the sea. The balloon soared to 12,000 feet. I'd only just learned to fly a balloon three weeks before." Near-death. Rescued by Royal Navy helicopter.

**1991** — First Pacific crossing by hot air balloon. Near-death — fuel tanks lost, needed to fly at 230mph, ended up in Canadian Arctic.
[一手] "The only thing that saw us was an otter that came up and gave us a sniff and then just walked off."

**1997–1998** — Attempted round-the-world balloon flight. Took off from Morocco, skirted Iraq (during US bombing campaign), nearly shot down over China.
[一手] "Tony Blair intervened. Just before we went into Chinese territory, they said we could get through quickly and they wouldn't shoot us down. As we flew over Shanghai, I got an email: 'The Chinese authorities have just given Virgin Atlantic permission to fly to Shanghai.'"

## 五、Virgin Empire Expansion (1993–2004)

**1993+** — Post-Records sale: rapid diversification using Virgin brand licensing model.
Virgin Megastores, Virgin Radio, Virgin Rail (1997), Virgin Mobile (1999), Virgin Blue (Australia, 2000), Virgin Active (health clubs), Virgin Money.

**1994** — Virgin Cola launched.
[一手] "I arrived in Times Square with a Sherman tank from Britain." Coke responded by kneecapping distribution. Failure by ~1998.
[推断] The Cola failure crystallized the "genuinely better" rule — challenger brand cannot win on personality alone against a category giant.

**1997** — Virgin Trains launches. Controversial — persistent service complaints.
[一手] "I was in the cinema in Zermatt with my children when the vibrating phone just kept going. The moment I knew the train had come off the tracks, whether it's your fault or not, you just get to the scene of the accident."

**1999** — Virgin Mobile launches in UK. Becomes successful challenger to incumbent telecoms.
[推断] Mobile fits the "ripped off customer" test — early mobile contracts were expensive and confusing. Virgin Mobile disrupted with simplicity and customer service.

## 六、Space & Late Period (2004–present)

**Early 1990s** — Gorbachev offers Branson a seat on a Russian rocket for $40–50 million. He declines.
[一手] "I felt it would be perceived as a waste of money for one individual to go to space. But it did get me thinking: why not build our own space line one day?"

**1999** — Registers "Virgin Galactic Airways" and "Virgin Intergalactic Airways."
[一手] "I'm an optimist."

**2004** — Virgin Galactic officially founded after SpaceShipOne wins the Ansari X Prize.
[一手] "We went out looking for a genius who could build a spaceship for us. We were lucky enough to come across Burt Rutan, who was that genius."

**2014 (October)** — Fatal SpaceShipTwo test accident. One test pilot killed, one seriously injured.
[一手] "We asked the 750 people who'd signed up to go to space — did they feel we should push on? Almost with one voice, they said we should. We asked the 700 engineers — did they want to push on? They, with one voice, said they did."
[二手] Program set back three years. All engineering brought in-house.

**2021 (July 11)** — Branson flies to space on VSS Unity. Nine days before Jeff Bezos's Blue Origin flight.
[二手] Media frames this as Branson "winning" the billionaire space race. Commercial flights follow in 2023.

**2023** — Virgin Galactic begins commercial spaceflights. Virgin Orbit (satellite launch) goes bankrupt.
[推断] The same year showing both the long game paying off (Galactic) and the capital-intensive risk catching up (Orbit).

## 七、Personal Milestones

**~1979** — Marries Joan Templeman (second wife, long-term partner before marriage). Children: Holly and Sam Branson.

**~1996** — Knighted (Sir Richard Branson).

**2006** — Publishes "Screw It, Let's Do It." Becomes one of his most-quoted works.

**2016 (August)** — Near-fatal cycling accident on Necker Island. Hospitalized with serious injuries.
[推断] This accelerated his shift toward the "business as a force for good" late-period philosophy — being close to death again sharpened the legacy question.

**2021** — COVID-era controversy: asked UK government for Virgin Atlantic bailout while resident in British Virgin Islands. Significant PR damage.

## 八、Chronological Arc — The Three Periods

| Period | Core identity | Driving force |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| 1966–1991 | The music entrepreneur | Build a label; records as the vehicle |
| 1984–2003 | The challenger brand builder | Attack industries where customers suffer |
| 2004–present | The adventurer-philanthropist | Space, climate, business as force for good |

[推断] The three periods overlap significantly — his adventurer identity ran throughout all three. But the center of gravity shifted from music to airlines/brand to space/philanthropy as he aged and as the stakes of each venture grew.
