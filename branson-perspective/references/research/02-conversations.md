### Pacific balloon crossing — near-death
[一手] "When we lost the fuel tanks, it looked like we didn't have a chance to survive. The chances were less than 1%. We needed a miracle to survive. We needed to go at speeds of over 200 miles an hour, and a balloon had never been faster than 80 miles an hour."

## 二、Virgin Atlantic Origin Story

[一手] "I was in Puerto Rico. I'd been bumped by American Airlines. I was about 28 years old. I went to the back of the airport and I borrowed a blackboard. I hired a plane. I wrote as a joke: 'Virgin Airlines, one way, $39 to the BVI.' I went around all the people who'd been bumped with me and I filled up my first plane."

[一手] "The next day I rang up Boeing and said, do you have any secondhand 747s for sale? The salesman said, whatever happens, Richard, change the name from Virgin — nobody will think a company with the name Virgin will go the whole way. So we didn't listen to him on that."

[一手] "The reason my partners were wrong was — traveling on airlines in those days was abysmal. They would dump a bit of cold chicken on your lap. There was no entertainment. The cabin crew didn't smile because they were treated so badly. So bring somebody in from the entertainment world and liven it up."

[一手] "The head of British Airways came out with a caustic comment: 'Too young to fly, too old to rock and roll. They're never going to succeed.'"

## 三、Virgin Records & Music Business

### Student magazine hustle (age 16)
[一手] "I was working out of the school phone box. I would keep ringing the operator and saying, 'I've been cut off,' and they would keep putting me through. So now I had my own secretary working for me, which was the British Telecom operator. And I wasn't paying for the phone calls."

[一手] "I learned early on that if you said to Coke that Pepsi were interested in taking an ad, they would jump in and say, 'We better take an ad too.' I learned the art of bull quite early on."

### John Lennon / Yoko Ono story
[一手] "Yoko Ono burst into tears. I said, what exactly is it? And they said, 'Oh, it's the heartbeat of Yoko Ono's dead baby that had died.' And that's all it was — it was just boom, boom, boom for four minutes. And I said, it's not really what we were expecting."

### Rolling Stones bid
[一手] "Prince Rupert said, if you can get me 6 million by Monday, I would seriously consider doing a deal with you. And he was only getting 3 million from other people. So on Thursday I flew off to Germany, France, Italy, as many countries as I could over the weekend, getting pledges of money. On Monday I rang him up and said we have your 6 million."

[推断] He got outbid that time — Prince Rupert just went to EMI and matched. But years later, the Rolling Stones signed with Virgin. Branson treats this as proof that persistence and relationship-building matter more than winning any individual negotiation.

## 四、British Airways "Dirty Tricks" Campaign

[一手] "British Airways launched what became famously known as the British Airways dirty tricks campaign. It was a kneecapping job to try to put Virgin Atlantic out of business completely."

[一手] "They would have a team of people behind locked doors ringing up our passengers, pretending to be from Virgin Atlantic, telling them the flight was delayed and then switching them onto British Airways."

[一手] "They had people intercepting our passengers at the airport. They had teams of people going through my rubbish bins. And if there was a journalist I'd talked to, they would go through the journalist's rubbish bins."

[一手] "We sued them for libel and we ended up winning the largest libel damages in British history. It was Christmas time, so we distributed them to all our staff equally — it became known as the 'British Airways Christmas bonus.' And all our staff were very happy."

[一手] "Your reputation is all you have in life. You've got to be very careful not to destroy that reputation by doing something that is verging on illegal — arguably was illegal."

## 五、Virgin Galactic & Space

[一手] "I got a telephone call and was told that President Gorbachev was on the phone. He invited me to go up on a Russian spaceship for something like 40 or 50 million dollars. But I felt it would be perceived as being a waste of money for one individual to go to space. I felt I could put that money to better use. But it did get me thinking: why not spend that money building our own space line one day?"

[一手] "I waited to see whether the American government, the Chinese government, the Russian government would be interested in putting you or me or people watching into space. It soon became apparent they had no interest in that whatsoever."

[一手] "I'm an optimist. I registered the name Virgin Galactic Airways. I also registered the name Virgin Intergalactic Airways."

### After the fatal test accident
[一手] "We asked the 750 people who'd signed up to go to space — did they feel we should push on? Almost with one voice, they said we should. We asked the 700 engineers — did they want to push on? They, with one voice, said they did."

[一手] "There's one moment in that journey where you go from nought miles an hour to over 3,000 miles an hour in seven seconds. And that's going to be some rush."

## 六、Business Philosophy in Conversation

### Decision-making process
[一手] "If I come up with an idea for a new business, I don't bring in accounting firms to do cost analysis. If I could create the kind of airline I'd want to fly on — exceptionally better than any other airline flying — it was likely that more money would come in than go out. And I'd have money left over at the end of the year. That turned out to be the case."

[一手] "My whole attitude to life is: if you create a new business that's the best in its field, it's likely to survive. When we took on Coca-Cola, a can of Virgin Cola and a can of Coke — ours may taste a little bit better, but we're not exceptionally better. And therefore it was easy for them to use their market dominance to put us out of business."

### On failure — Virgin Cola
[一手] "I suspect the most notable failure has been not knocking Coca-Cola off their pedestal. We had a good try for a year or two. It looked like we were going to take Coke for everything they had."

[一手] "I then got a bit too big for my boots and I arrived in Times Square with a Sherman tank from Britain. I crushed all these Coca-Cola and Pepsi cans. Coke didn't take that too kindly. The next day in Atlanta, they got bags of suitcases of money, got a DC-10 on the runway, filled it with squads, arrived in England, and retailers suddenly became very wealthy and all our Virgin Cola disappeared off the shelves."

### On being positive
[一手] "I look at everything as an opportunity. I'm positive about life generally. I'm definitely a glass nearly full kind of person rather than a glass half full."

## 七、Personal/Childhood Stories

### Mother pushing him out of the car at age 4
[一手] "She was the kind of mother that felt we needed to stand on our own two feet. She would do things which most likely she would get arrested for today. I got lost in the fields and ended up being rescued by a farmer. She did claim she was honking her horn as it started to get dark."

[一手] "Even at four, I was thinking — damn you, I'm going to make you sweat."

### Leaving school at 16
[一手] "It took three walks around the lawn. The first walk, my dad said you should really try to finish your education. The second way around the garden, he started to mellow. On the third walk, he said, 'Well, at least you know what you want to do. Go and give it a go.' They were very loving, very understanding parents."

### Dyslexia — net vs gross story
[一手] "It wasn't until I was 50 that I was in a board meeting and one of the directors said, 'Richard, you just said is that good news — did you not realize whether that was your profit or your turnover?' So he took a sheet of paper, penciled it blue like the sea, put a fishing net in it, put fish in the net. He said, 'Your profit at the end of the year — that's your net profit. The rest is your turnover.' And I went, hey presto. I know the difference between net and gross."

[一手] "I had the biggest private group of companies in Europe and I did not know the difference between net and gross."

[一手] "I think being dyslexic by and large helps people. The great thing about being dyslexic is you concentrate on the things you're good at and you often excel at those things. I had to become a good delegator very early on. Most likely Virgin, which now employs 90,000 people, would not have been successful if I hadn't been dyslexic."

## 八、On Crisis Leadership

[一手] "I was in the cinema in Zermatt with my children and this vibrating phone just kept going in my pocket. I looked at the phone. The moment I knew the train had come off the tracks — whether it's your fault or not, you just get to the scene of the accident."

[一手] "There was an elderly lady who had been killed. I went to the morgue to meet her children. We all had a big hug. Then I went to meet the press in the field. If there's a bad incident, the critical thing is to confront it head on, and make sure you're there to comfort anybody who's affected."
