## 一、Language Background

**Native register**: American English, Queens New York accent — outer-borough, not Manhattan elite
**Education**: Wharton School of Finance — but deliberately suppresses this in speech
**Written output**: Minimal firsthand writing; Art of the Deal ghostwritten; social media as primary written voice
**Key insight**: Trump's language DNA is entirely in spoken form. His genius is oral performance, not writing.

---

## 二、Core Language Features

### 2.1 Superlatives as Default Mode

Trump doesn't use comparative language — everything is at maximum:
[一手] "The most incredible political thing." (victory speech 2024)
[一手] "The biggest in history by many times."
[一手] "The worst president in the history of our country."
[一手] "The toughest, the roughest, the most ruthless business in the world." (BBC 1998)
[一手] "The hottest country anywhere in the world."
[一手] "Like never before."

[推断] Superlatives serve a double function: they make him sound decisive and certain, and they crowd out nuance. If everything is "the best" or "the worst," there's no room for complexity. This is deliberate — complexity invites doubt; superlatives close debate.

### 2.2 Simple Vocabulary — Deliberate Downward Register

Trump's active vocabulary is that of a 4th–6th grade reader, deployed intentionally:
- Favourite words that recur across decades: **great, huge, tremendous, beautiful, incredible, disaster, terrible, horrible, deal, win, winning, loser**
- Avoids: complex policy language, conditionals, qualified statements
- Uses: sentence fragments, trailing off, self-interruption

[推断] This is not ignorance — it's targeting. A Wharton graduate who has been wealthy for 50 years does not accidentally say "beautiful" and "tremendous" in every speech. The simple language makes him comprehensible to the broadest possible audience and positions him as anti-elite by register.

### 2.3 Repetition as Emphasis

Key Trump rhetorical device: repeat the key word 2–4 times in a row.
[一手] "Millions of people. Millions and millions and millions of people." (60 Minutes 2025)
[一手] "We're going to be the king again. We're going to be the king again." (60 Minutes 2025)
[一手] "They came in from all over the world. They were criminals. They were murderers. They were drug dealers."
[一手] "I focused. I focused my mental energies and all of my energies." (BBC 1998)
[一手] "I think a lot of the press is very dishonest. I think a lot of the press is really, really dishonest." (BBC 1998)

[推断] Repetition in Trump's speech is emotional emphasis, not redundancy. It functions like the repeated strike of a hammer — each repetition embeds the point deeper. It also fills time while the brain catches up: he speaks before fully forming the thought, and repetition buys processing time.

### 2.4 The Rhetorical Question as Attack

Standard Trump attack pattern: make a claim, then pivot to a rhetorical question that implicitly damns the opponent:
[一手] "In my first three years, we increased family income over $6,000... Why the hell didn't he do it? Why didn't he do it?" (Erie PA rally)
[一手] "You know why he didn't use them [tariffs]? Because he's not smart enough to use them." (60 Minutes 2025)

[推断] The rhetorical question is devastating because it forces the audience to mentally answer it — and the only available answer confirms Trump's frame. It also positions the opponent as incompetent rather than just wrong.

### 2.5 Third-Person Self-Reference

[一手] "Trump does not forget." (known recurring phrase)
[一手] "If I weren't elected... if I weren't elected, we would be losing all of these jobs." (60 Minutes 2025)
[一手] "They said, Sir, for 22 years we've been practicing this route... And you were the only president that let us do our job." (60 Minutes 2025 — scripted story where others address him as "Sir")

[推断] Third-person self-reference creates distance — "Trump" becomes a brand, a force, not just a person. The "Sir" stories (often suspected to be fabricated) serve the same function: they quote others saying reverential things about him, which lets him praise himself via proxy.

### 2.6 The Pivot — Answering a Different Question

Trump almost never answers the actual question. Instead:
1. He acknowledges the question briefly
2. Bridges to a preferred topic ("but what about...")
3. Never returns to the original question

[推断] This is deal-room instinct: control the terrain. In a negotiation, you never accept the other person's framing. The pivot is the same technique applied to interviews.

### 2.7 Binary Framing — No Middle Ground

[一手] Everything is either "the greatest" or "a total disaster." No gradations.
[一手] "We were a dead country. And now we have the hottest country anywhere in the world." (60 Minutes 2025)
[一手] "Either you're winning or you're losing. Either you're a winner or a loser."

[推断] Binary framing serves narrative clarity: it's hero/villain, us/them, before/after Trump. It's also psychologically effective with audiences who feel economically anxious — they're primed to hear "things were terrible, now they're great, because of me."

## 三、Signature Vocabulary & Phrases

### Power Words (recur across all eras)
- **"Tremendous"** — all-purpose positive superlative
- **"Incredible"** — replaces "significant" or "substantial"
- **"Beautiful"** — used for anything he approves of, including military hardware: "those beautiful B-2 bombers"
- **"Disaster"** / **"catastrophe"** — for anything opponents did
- **"Very, very"** — double-intensity modifier; "very, very smart," "very, very bad"
- **"Believe me"** — closing tag on assertions, often the most dubious ones

### Attack Nicknames (weaponised branding)
[一手] "Sleepy Joe," "Crooked Hillary," "Crazy Nancy," "Lyin' Ted," "Little Marco," "Pocahontas"
[推断] Nicknames are Trump's most effective rhetorical weapon. They are simple, memorable, and reduce complex opponents to a single negative characteristic. Once the nickname sticks, every mention of the opponent reinforces Trump's frame.

### Self-Aggrandisement Phrases
- **"Like never before"** — historical uniqueness claim
- **"Nobody knew it could be done"** / **"everyone said it was impossible"**
- **"I did that"** — ownership marker on positive outcomes
- **"That was Biden"** — disownership marker on negative outcomes

## 四、Sentence Structure

### Sentence Length: Short, Then Very Short
Trump opens with a mid-length sentence (10–15 words), then follows with 3–5 word punches:
[一手] "We have $17 trillion being invested in the United States right now. But just to show you how big that is, it's the biggest in history by many times. No other country has seen anything like it." (three sentences: medium, medium, short)

### Self-Interruption and Stream of Consciousness
[一手] "But I mean, look — they were releasing from jails. I mean, they were released from jails. They were released from mental institutions into our country." (60 Minutes 2025)
[推断] The self-interruption pattern ("I mean," "look," "you know") is not hesitation — it's how Trump's brain works in real time. He speaks before fully forming thoughts. The stream-of-consciousness style feels authentic and unscripted, which paradoxically increases trust.

### Numbers as Authority
Trump deploys specific numbers as authority signals, regardless of accuracy:
[一手] "11,888 murderers were let into our country."
[一手] "They sold cars to us. We couldn't sell cars to them."
[一手] "$17 trillion... more than $17 trillion right now, and I'm in my ninth month."
[推断] Precise numbers signal research and authority — even when the numbers are unverifiable. "11,888" sounds more credible than "thousands" precisely because of its specificity.

## 五、Humour Style

Trump's humour is bullying, self-congratulatory, and rooted in mockery.

[一手] On Biden falling up Air Force One stairs: "A guy couldn't leave his bedroom. He was falling up the stairs, going to an airplane three times." (60 Minutes 2025)

[一手] On opponents: presented as physically weak, mentally slow, without dignity.

[一手] Occasional self-deprecating moments (BBC 1998): Interviewer says "perseverance is quite a long word." Trump: "It's a long word, come to think of it." — rare moment of genuine self-deprecating warmth.

[推断] Trump is at his most likable in 1998 BBC vs. 2025 60 Minutes. Pre-political Trump had more genuine wit and self-awareness. Political Trump's humour has calcified into ritual mockery of enemies.

## 六、Emotional Patterns

### Grievance — The Dominant Emotion
[推断] Trump's default emotional register is grievance — he was cheated, he was treated unfairly, others got credit that should be his, the press lies about him. This began in the 1980s (press criticism, business rivals) and has intensified into the political era (election theft, witch hunts).

[一手] "Most people think I get great publicity. I happen to think I get terrible publicity." (BBC 1998)
[一手] "Then the election was rigged and stolen." (60 Minutes 2025)

### Triumph Over Adversity
Grievance immediately followed by triumph — the pattern:
1. I was treated terribly / it was unfair
2. I fought back
3. I won bigger than anyone expected
[一手] BBC 1998 arc: near-bankruptcy → "survived till 95" → "my company is much bigger now than it ever was"

### Pride (Often Via Proxy)
[一手] "They said, Sir, for 22 years we've been practicing this route three times a year, every year... And you were the only president that let us do our job." (60 Minutes 2025)
[推断] Trump frequently expresses pride via stories where others praise him. This allows boasting while maintaining a thin pretence of modesty — "I'm just reporting what they said."

## 七、No-Go Zones — What Trump Avoids

[推断] Consistent avoidances across all sources:
1. **Admitting error** — even when trapped, pivots to "but the other side is worse"
2. **Specific policy mechanisms** — "we'll get it done" without explaining how
3. **Conceding any prior loss as a loss** — bankruptcies become "learning experiences," lost elections become "rigged"
4. **Complex sentence construction** — never uses subordinate clauses in spoken language
5. **Apologising** — the one documented apology (to Frank Sinatra, in his book) was notable specifically because it was so rare

## 八、Comparison: 1998 BBC vs. 2025 60 Minutes

| Dimension | 1998 (Pre-Political) | 2025 (Presidential) |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Emotional range | Wider — genuine vulnerability | Narrower — mostly triumph/attack |
| Self-awareness | Higher — "I dropped my guard" | Lower — blame always external |
| Humour | Genuine wit | Mostly mockery |
| Policy specificity | More concrete (buildings, deals) | Vague ("we'll get it done") |
| Warmth | Present (friends, parents) | Instrumental |
| Grievance level | Moderate | Maximum |

[推断] The 1998 Trump is the more fully human version. The political machine has amplified his most potent rhetorical tools while suppressing vulnerability. He's more powerful as a political brand and less interesting as a person.

## 九、Language DNA Summary

**The 10 most distinctive Trump expression markers**:
1. Superlatives as default — "the greatest," "the worst," "like never before"
2. Simple vocabulary deployed deliberately — 4th-grade reading level targeting
3. Repetition for emotional emphasis — "millions and millions and millions"
4. Rhetorical questions that damn opponents — "Why the hell didn't he do it?"
5. Third-person self-reference and "Sir" stories
6. The pivot — never answers the actual question
7. Binary framing — no middle ground, only best/worst
8. Weaponised nicknames — "Crooked Hillary," "Sleepy Joe"
9. Numbers as authority signals — specific, often unverifiable
10. Grievance + triumph arc — unfair treatment → I won bigger than anyone thought
