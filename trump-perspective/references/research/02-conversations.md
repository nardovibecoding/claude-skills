## 一、BBC HardTalk 1998 — Pre-Political Trump

**Context**: 1998 interview. Trump at 51, post-bankruptcy recovery, pre-Apprentice. The most unguarded version of Trump on record — no political handlers, no campaign discipline. Pure dealmaker mode.

### On Business Philosophy
[一手] "Well, I'm not sure that I've actually used those words [shake things up], but generally you have to shake things up pretty much in order to do something of consequence."

[一手] "I think you have to be smart in business. I don't think you have to be a killer. I think you have to be smart." (but then immediately adds:) "One of the things I say in the book very strongly: you have to be paranoid. There is a certain advantage to having a certain degree of paranoia. You watch what's happening behind your back."

[一手] On what drives him: "I really think it's the artistic or the aesthetic. I love building great buildings. I get a great sense of artistic enjoyment out of those buildings."

[一手] "I get great artistic pride out of a great building like Trump Tower."

### On Getting Even
[一手] "I believe strongly in getting even. If somebody has hurt you, if somebody's gone out of their way to hurt you, I think that if you have the opportunity, you should certainly go out of your way to do a number on them. And I've had more criticism about that one statement in my book than any other statement. The clergy called — the ministers, the priests, the rabbis — they've all said, what a terrible thing to say. That's against our teachings. I just believe it. I believe in an eye for an eye."

[一手] "There were people that I really helped in business when things were very good in the 1980s, and they did not lift a finger to help me when I needed it. Now I have the opportunity to do a number on those people, and I will tell you I'm having a lot of fun with the opportunity."

### On the 1990s Bankruptcy
[一手] "I had faced the possibility of losing everything. And I went back to work. I focused. I focused my mental energies and all of my energies."

[一手] "I think one of the reasons I really succeeded — bigger than even in the 80s — is the fact that it's a little word called perseverance. I didn't stop."

[一手] "I came up with a phrase: survive till 95. That was in the early 1990s. And it turned out to be right because the world changed."

[一手] On dropping his guard pre-crisis: "I was having too good a time. I was enjoying my life too much. Things were going too well. I did drop my guard."

[一手] On people who abandoned him: "I wrote once that I would love to sort of have a bad period financially — just to see who my friends would be. Now I never write it again because it's not fun. And it might be a self-fulfilling prophecy."

### On Money
[一手] "Again, money does not buy happiness... but it certainly makes life easier, and it really does create — it lets me create what I want to do artistically because I'm an artist. In a certain sense, I'm an artist."

[一手] "I happen to be the biggest developer in New York."

### On Press
[一手] "I think a lot of the press is very dishonest. I think a lot of the press is really, really dishonest."

[一手] "Most people think I get great publicity. I happen to think I get terrible publicity."

[一手] On not caring about press: "The one thing I have learned — it's a one-week phenomenon. It doesn't matter. At the end of a week, usually at the end of a day, it's totally gone. You don't even remember the story. And once you get that into your head, you can live with it."

### On Women
[一手] "I believe that women are actually stronger than men. I think they're more aggressive than men."

[一手] "Prenuptial agreements are ugly, vicious, terrible documents that you have to have."

[一手] On relationships and success: "My thing is I'm thinking about deals and I'm thinking about these great buildings. There's a lot of things that I'm doing and building. And I'm thinking about that maybe as opposed to relationship... success may be great in terms of living and lifestyle and beautiful homes and apartments and boats and planes and all of the stuff that doesn't mean very much. But success is not necessarily very good for a relationship."

## 二、60 Minutes Overtime 2025 — Presidential Trump

**Context**: Recorded in Trump's second term. Interviewed by Nora O'Donnell. Trump at the height of second-term confidence. Very different register from 1998 — more combative, more grandiose, still the same core patterns.

### On Trade and Tariffs
[一手] "Tariffs are incredible because they really give us great national security... Tariffs give us national security and great wealth."

[一手] "I immediately instituted a 100 percent tariff over and above what they were already paying. And when I did that, they immediately came to the table. Without tariffs, we would not have had that privilege."

[一手] "We have $17 trillion being invested in the United States right now... it's the biggest in history by many times. No other country has seen anything like it. The Biden administration in four years did less than a trillion."

[一手] On tariff pressure with China: "I said, look, if you don't open up, then what we're going to do is we're going to impose a hundred percent tariff over and above what you're already paying. And as soon as I said that, they came to the table... We got a phone call 20 minutes later. We'd like to talk."

### On Geopolitics
[一手] On Taiwan: "You'll find out if it happens. And he [Xi] understands the answer to that... I can't give away my secrets. I don't want to be one of these guys that tells you exactly what's going to happen."

[一手] On Putin/Ukraine: "That was a war that would have never happened if I was president... That's Joe Biden's war, not my war. I inherited that stupid war."

[一手] On nuclear testing: "We have enough nuclear weapons to blow up the world 150 times... If other people are going to have them, we're going to have to have them. And if we have them, we have to test them."

[一手] On solving wars: "I solved eight of them. Those are eight of the nine wars I solved... You know how I solved them? I said, in many cases, in 60 percent, I said, if you don't stop fighting, I'm putting tariffs on both of your countries, and you're not going to be able to do business with the United States."

### On Biden
[一手] "Joe Biden is the worst president in the history of our country. We had the worst inflation. We had the worst severity of them... The worst thing he did is allow millions of people to come into our country, unvetted, unchecked. And they were criminals. They were murderers."

[一手] "Barack Hussein Obama, who is a lousy president, not nearly as bad as Biden."

[一手] "Had Kamala won the election, our country right now would be finished. It was a dead country. And now we have the hottest country anywhere in the world."

### On Immigration
[一手] "Have some of these raids gone too far?" [Interviewer] "No, I think they haven't gone far enough, because we've been held back by the judges."

[一手] "The policy has to be: you came into the country illegally, you're going to go out."

### On Democrats
[一手] "They've become crazed lunatics... There's something wrong with these people."

[一手] "The Democrats are vicious. They're vicious. They have horrible policy, but they are the most vicious human beings on earth. They are people that weaponize government."

## 三、The Apprentice Era — "You're Fired" as Performance

**Context**: 14 seasons (2004–2015). Trump performing authority for 40 million viewers per episode at peak. The show was the single greatest brand rehabilitation in American media history.

[二手] The phrase "You're fired" — delivered from behind a boardroom table, pointed finger — became Trump's signature. More Americans knew Trump from The Apprentice than from his actual businesses.

[推断] The Apprentice era is crucial: it taught Trump that performance of competence works better than actual competence. He played a decisive billionaire boss, and the country believed it. The political career is a direct extension of this playbook — same character, larger stage.

## 四、2015 Presidential Announcement — Setting the Template

**Context**: Escalator descent at Trump Tower, June 2015.

[一手] "When Mexico sends its people, they're not sending their best... They're bringing drugs. They're bringing crime. They're rapists. And some, I assume, are good people."

[推断] This was the founding rhetorical act of the political career. It established: binary framing (us/them), institutional attack (Mexico, politicians), and a promise of strength. The "some, I assume, are good people" hedge became a recurring ironic pattern — massive provocation followed by small qualification.

## 五、Conversation Patterns — Cross-Source Analysis

### Deflection and Pivot
[推断] Trump almost never answers the exact question asked. He answers the question he wants to answer. Classic example from 60 Minutes 2025:
- Interviewer: "Would you order US forces to defend Taiwan?"
- Trump: "You'll find out if it happens. And he understands the answer to that."
The pivot preserves leverage.

### Crediting Himself, Blaming Predecessors
[推断] Virtually every positive statistic is "because of me / because of tariffs / because of what I did." Every negative is "because of Biden / Obama / the Democrats." This is completely consistent across 1998 (BBC) and 2025 (60 Minutes) — a 27-year unchanged pattern.

### The List Instinct
[一手] In 60 Minutes 2025, Trump literally produced a written list of 8 wars he claims to have resolved. The instinct: volume proves scale; showing a list is more persuasive than explaining any single item.
