## 二、关键投资决策

### 1. 年龄11岁 — Cities Service Preferred (1942)
**What happened**: Bought 3 shares at $38 ($114.75 total — "every penny I'd saved"). Watched it drop to $27, held. Sold at $40, a modest gain. It then rose to $200.

[一手] "I put my whole net worth in, and since March 11th, 1942, I have never had less than 80% of my money in American business."

**The lesson learned**: Patience. Selling too early became the formative mistake — the first encounter with the cost of impatience.

[推断] Every subsequent Buffett principle — patience, holding forever, not watching the tape — traces back to this single trade at age 11. The $160 he missed on those 3 shares was worth billions in lessons.

---

### 2. 1950 — Columbia University, Ben Graham
**What happened**: At 19, read "The Intelligent Investor." Enrolled at Columbia specifically to study under Graham. Intellectual foundation reset.

[一手] "I had the whole wrong idea. I thought stocks were things that went up and down, and I charted them. I read books on technical analysis. Then I read Ben Graham when I was 19 or 20, and I realized that I was doing it exactly the wrong way. I rejiggered my mind."

[一手] "From that point, I never bought another stock. I bought businesses that happened to be publicly traded. I became an owner of a business."

**Significance**: The conversion from technical trader to business owner. Everything else flows from this reorientation.

---

### 3. 1956 — Buffett Partnership Ltd.
**What happened**: Returned to Omaha with $174,000 (including $100 borrowed from father). Formed partnership with 7 family/friends. $105,000 initial capital.

[一手] "We formed the first partnership 50 years ago. $105,000. That's my sister clapping — she was in the partnership." — AGM reference

**Performance**: Compounded at ~31% annually over 13 years. This is the record that built his reputation before Berkshire.

[推断] The partnership era demonstrated what he could do with small capital. The constraint of scale is what made the post-1969 Berkshire era harder — but the principles didn't change. He would "probably be 100 percent in Korea" with small capital today.

---

### 4. 1965 — Berkshire Hathaway acquisition
**What happened**: Bought a failing New England textile mill after the seller lowballed him by 1/8th of a dollar on a promised buyback price. Out of spite, Buffett launched a takeover instead.

[推断] He has acknowledged this was one of his worst decisions: he overpaid (emotionally) for a terrible business in a dying industry. It took 20 years to close the mills. The cost was the opportunity cost of deploying that capital elsewhere.

**Why it became great anyway**: He recognized the error and pivoted — using Berkshire's cash flows and insurance float to buy great businesses instead of reinvesting in textiles. The terrible acquisition became the vehicle for everything.

---

### 5. 1969 — Dissolved Partnership
**What happened**: Returned all capital to partners. Market was overvalued; he couldn't find enough value. Took a rare pause.

[推断] This is underappreciated in the Buffett story. Most investors never know when to stop. The discipline to say "I cannot find enough to do" and return capital — rather than chase returns in an overvalued market — reflects a depth of conviction rare even among value investors.

---

### 6. 1972 — See's Candies ($25 million)
**What happened**: Munger convinced Buffett to buy See's Candies at a significant premium to book value — unusual for a Graham-trained investor. See's had a brand, pricing power, and required minimal capital reinvestment.

[二手] Munger's lesson: "Forget about ever buying another company like Berkshire. Add to it wonderful companies purchased at fair prices."

**The lesson**: Brand moats are more valuable than hard assets. See's could raise prices every year with almost no capital expenditure. This insight — that you're buying earnings power, not balance sheet assets — transformed Berkshire's entire subsequent strategy.

[推断] See's Candies is the hinge point. Before See's: Graham-style asset value hunting. After See's: brand/franchise hunting. Coca-Cola, Gillette, American Express, Apple all flow from the See's insight.

---

### 7. 1988 — Coca-Cola ($1 billion)
**What happened**: Deployed $1 billion into Coca-Cola at a then-alarming valuation. Largest investment Berkshire had ever made.

[一手] "Be fearful when others are greedy and greedy only when others are fearful." — the principle, though this purchase was not during a panic but a recognition of brand quality

**Why it mattered**: Validated brand-moat investing at scale. Coke has a global distribution system, unmatched brand recognition, and the ability to raise prices without losing volume. The investment grew >10x and still throws off massive dividends.

---

### 8. 2008 — Goldman Sachs ($5 billion preferred + warrants)
**What happened**: During the height of the 2008 financial crisis, Goldman needed both capital and credibility. Buffett committed $5 billion in preferred shares, with warrants to buy common stock at $115.

[一手] "I said, we'll give you $10 billion under these circumstances. But we'll do it. We'll do it tomorrow." — on the Occidental deal; same decisive style applied to Goldman

[一手] "They had to have somebody who counted besides people whose position it was to lie." — why Goldman needed Berkshire's credibility, not just the cash

[推断] This was "be greedy when others are fearful" in its most visible form. The terms were extremely favorable to Berkshire (10% preferred dividend + warrants). Buffett made an estimated $3B+ profit. More importantly, the act sealed his reputation as the lender of last resort to American capitalism.

---

### 9. 2016 — Apple
**What happened**: Broke his long-standing "no tech" rule. Began buying Apple stock. By 2023, Apple was Berkshire's single largest position.

[推断] He didn't change his framework — he changed his category. Apple is not a tech company in his view; it is a consumer brand with an iPhone as a habit-forming product. The moat is customer loyalty and ecosystem lock-in, not technology innovation. See's Candies logic applied at $2 trillion scale.

**Significance**: Proved his circle of competence could expand. Also proved he wasn't dogmatic — when the facts changed and his understanding deepened, he acted.

---

### 10. 2019 — Occidental Petroleum ($10 billion)
**What happened**: Received a call on a Friday. Met Sunday in his office. Committed $10 billion to finance Occidental's acquisition of Anadarko.

[一手] "I said, we'll give you $10 billion under these circumstances. But we'll do it. We'll do it tomorrow. And they knew I was good for it."

[推断] The deal structure: preferred shares with 8% dividend + warrants to buy common stock. Berkshire's advantage is the ability to say yes immediately, with certainty, and in enormous size. No investment bank can replicate that — they need committees, approvals, roadshows. He needs one call.

---

### 11. 2020 — Airlines exit (COVID)
**What happened**: Sold all airline holdings at significant losses during COVID. Had owned Delta, American, United, Southwest.

[一手 / 二手] He acknowledged this was likely a mistake in hindsight but defended the decision as appropriate given the uncertainty about airline futures.

[推断] The airlines exit illustrates an important principle: "our favorite holding period is forever" has a limit — businesses that may be permanently impaired by structural change are not forever businesses. He drew the same lesson: bought them thinking he understood the business; decided he didn't anymore.

## 三、犯错模式分析

[一手] "The mistakes we made are of two kinds. One is when we didn't invest at all in something we understood that was cheap. The second was starting in on something that could have been a very large investment and not maximizing it."

**Named regrets:**
- Walmart ($10B in opportunity cost — sold too early when price moved against him by $1)
- Dexter Shoe (paid in Berkshire stock for a business that was destroyed by Chinese imports; doubly costly because the stock given up appreciated enormously)
- Not buying Amazon sooner
- Airlines (acknowledged the framework error of thinking he understood the business)

[一手] "It doesn't bother us. You have to hit a few in the woods occasionally just to make it a little more interesting."

[推断] Buffett's equanimity about errors is not stoicism — it is structural. He runs a diversified enough portfolio that no single mistake is lethal. The Berkshire model is designed to survive mistakes, not to avoid all of them.

## 四、决策风格特征

**Speed**: Can decide in 5 minutes if the opportunity is within his circle
**Concentration**: Has no problem with 80-100% in one position (demonstrated at age 11)
**Temperament**: Does not let Mr. Market instruct him; uses price drops as buying opportunities
**Size**: "When I want to do something, I always want to do it big"
**Terms**: Always insists on his terms; no negotiation on the fundamental structure; take-it-or-leave-it
**Cash**: Always maintains enormous liquidity to be the buyer when others must sell
