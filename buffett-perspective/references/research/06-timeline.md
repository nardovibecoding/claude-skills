## 一、早年与教育 (1930–1955)

**1930-08-30** — Born in Omaha, Nebraska. Father: Howard Buffett, stockbroker and congressman. Midwestern Protestant middle-class household.

**1930s (childhood)** — Grows up reading his father's financial books. Begins following the stock market before age 10.

**1936 (age 6)** — Begins selling Juicy Fruit gum door-to-door. First entrepreneurial venture.

**1941 (age 11)** — Reads every book on the stock market in the Omaha Public Library. Some twice.
[一手] "I've read every book in the Omaha Public Library that was on the stock market. By 11, I'll guarantee you."

**1942-03-11 (age 11)** — First stock purchase: 3 shares of Cities Service Preferred at $38/share ($114.75 total — every penny saved). Watches it fall to $27; holds. Sells at $40 for a small gain. Stock subsequently rises to $200.
[一手] "Since March 11th, 1942, I have never had less than 80% of my money in American business."

**1944–1945** — Files first tax return (age 14); claims $35 deduction for bicycle used in paper route.

**1947** — Graduates from Woodrow Wilson High School in Washington D.C. (father was serving in Congress). Voted "Most Likely to Succeed."

**1947–1949** — Attends University of Pennsylvania (Wharton). Transfers to University of Nebraska. Graduates in 3 years.

**1950** — Reads "The Intelligent Investor" by Benjamin Graham. Intellectual conversion: stops charting stocks, starts thinking as a business owner.
[一手] "I rejiggered my mind when I read the book, Intelligent Investor. From that point, I never bought another stock. I bought businesses that happened to be publicly traded."

**1950–1951** — Applies to Harvard Business School; rejected. Enrolls at Columbia Business School specifically to study under Ben Graham. Earns the only A+ Graham ever gave.

**1951–1954** — Works briefly in Omaha as a stockbroker. Writes investment articles. Builds early personal capital (~$10,000 by 1951, $140,000 by 1955).

## 二、合伙人阶段与早期声誉 (1956–1969)

**1956-05-04** — Founds Buffett Partnership Ltd. with $105,000 from 7 limited partners (family and friends). His own contribution: $100. Takes no management fee; takes 25% of profits above 6% hurdle rate.

**1957–1968** — Partnership compounds at ~31% annually over 13 years vs. ~9% for the Dow. Generates enormous returns for early partners.

**1958** — Purchases family home in Omaha for $31,500. Still lives there in 2026.
[一手] "I've lived in the same house since 1958. Something like 63 or 64 years. A quarter of the life of the country."

**1959** — Meets Charlie Munger at a dinner in Omaha. Instant intellectual chemistry. Described later as the most important meeting of his business life.

**1961** — Partnership holds ~10% of Berkshire Fine Spinning Associates (textile manufacturer).

**1962** — Opens office at Kiewit Plaza, Omaha. Same building, different floor, to the present day.
[一手] "I've gone to the same office since January of '62. So it's been 60 years."

**1962-01** — Begins buying Berkshire Hathaway stock aggressively at under $8/share (book value ~$19).

**1965** — After seller Seabury Stanton lowballs Buffett by 1/8th of a dollar on a promised share buyback, Buffett launches a full takeover out of spite. Takes control of Berkshire. Later describes this as one of his most expensive emotional decisions.

**1965–1969** — Continues running both the partnership and Berkshire. Begins shifting Berkshire's capital from textiles to investments.

**1966** — Buys Associated Cotton Shops (retail). First non-textile Berkshire subsidiary.

**1967** — Berkshire enters insurance via National Indemnity Co. The insurance float model — float = money held before paying claims, used to invest — becomes the financial engine of everything that follows.

**1969** — Dissolves Buffett Partnership Ltd. Returns all capital to partners. Explains: market is overvalued, can't find enough attractive investments. Partners keep their Berkshire shares.

## 三、Berkshire 转型与见闻年 (1970–1988)

**1970** — Becomes chairman of Berkshire Hathaway. Begins writing annual letters to shareholders. These will continue for 55+ years.

**1972** — Buys See's Candies for $25 million (a then-significant premium to book value). Charlie Munger persuades him. Transformative insight: brand moats and pricing power matter more than asset values.

**1973–1974** — Stock market crashes 50%. Buffett is excited, not scared. Deploys capital aggressively into Washington Post, other undervalued businesses.

**1977** — First formal annual shareholder letter (prior letters exist but 1977 is the canonical start of the public record at berkshirehathaway.com). Meets Carol Loomis of Fortune, who begins editing the letters — a partnership that lasts 45+ years.

**1979** — Berkshire stock hits $290/share (up from ~$8 in 1962).

**1983** — Buys Nebraska Furniture Mart from Rose Blumkin ("Mrs. B"). Classic Buffett deal: extraordinary family business, autonomous management, permanent capital.

**1985** — Closes the original Berkshire textile mills. 20 years of keeping failing mills running; eventually admits the error and exits.

**1985–1986** — Berkshire enters Cap Cities/ABC deal alongside Tom Murphy. Learns from great media operators; deepens understanding of media moats.

**1988** — Begins buying Coca-Cola. Spends $1 billion over two years. At the time, the largest investment Berkshire has made.

## 四、全球化与鼎盛期 (1989–2008)

**1989** — Publishes the "owner's manual" principles in Berkshire's annual report. The most clear articulation of Berkshire's management philosophy.

**1990s** — Berkshire stock crosses $10,000/share. Buffett refuses to split, reasoning that high share price attracts long-term owners rather than speculators.

**1991** — Salomon Brothers scandal. Buffett steps in as interim chairman to manage the crisis. Testifies before Congress. Classic line: "Lose money for the firm, and I will be understanding. Lose a shred of reputation for the firm, and I will be ruthless."
[一手] "It takes 20 years to build a reputation and 5 minutes to ruin it."

**1996** — Creates Berkshire Class B shares at 1/30th of Class A price (later split 50:1 in 2010). Makes Berkshire accessible to smaller investors.

**1998** — Acquires General Re ($22 billion in Berkshire stock). Largest acquisition yet. Also acquires Executive Jet (NetJets). Scale is now beyond what the old partnership would recognize.

**1999** — Berkshire underperforms S&P 500 sharply as dot-com bubble inflates. Critics say Buffett "doesn't get" the new economy. He refuses to buy tech stocks he doesn't understand.

**2000** — Dot-com bubble bursts. Buffett's restraint vindicated. Berkshire outperforms for the next several years.

**2002** — LTCM/junk bond crisis creates opportunities. Buys junk bonds heavily while others are paralyzed.
[一手] "You didn't have to have lots of investment smarts to buy junk bonds in 2002. You really just had to have the courage of your convictions."

**2006** — Announces the Giving Pledge: will donate 99% of his wealth, primarily to the Gates Foundation. Begins annual donations of Berkshire B shares.

**2007–2008** — Financial crisis. Buffett invests $5B in Goldman Sachs preferred shares (September 2008) and $3B in GE preferred. Both at extraordinarily favorable terms.
[一手] "They had to have somebody who counted besides people whose position it was to lie."

## 五、晚年与传承 (2009–2026)

**2009–2015** — Berkshire buys BNSF Railway ($44 billion, 2009); IBM (~$10 billion); various energy businesses. Elephant-hunting mode.

**2010** — Berkshire Class B stock splits 50:1 as part of Burlington Northern acquisition.

**2011** — Berkshire acquires 700+ businesses and subsidiaries. Annual meeting attendance exceeds 40,000.

**2013–2016** — Underperforms S&P 500 again as growth stocks dominate. Critics renew "Buffett is outdated" narrative.

**2016** — Begins buying Apple stock. Eventually becomes Berkshire's largest single position. The "no tech" era ends.
[推断] The Apple bet is the most important late-career decision: proof that principles (brand moat, pricing power, consumer loyalty) can be applied to technology companies, not just candy and Coke.

**2019** — $10 billion commitment to Occidental Petroleum preferred shares. Characteristic Sunday deal: call received, terms agreed, done.

**2020 (COVID)** — Sells all airline holdings at losses. Holds enormous cash position when deal flow dries up. AGM goes virtual. Unusual passivity during the March 2020 crash (later admits he should have bought more).

**2020–2022** — Begins buying Japanese trading houses (Itochu, Marubeni, Mitsubishi, Mitsui, Sumitomo). Unexpected international move; all companies fit the "good business at fair price" framework.

**2022** — Berkshire B shares hit all-time high. Accelerates Occidental buying. Stock repurchases exceed $7 billion.

**2023-11-28** — Charlie Munger dies at age 99. One month before his 100th birthday.

**2023** — Annual letter is extended tribute to Munger. [二手] Described as the most personal thing Buffett has ever published.

**2024** — Final year as CEO (succession to Greg Abel announced as complete). 2024 letter frames the transition; Berkshire described as built to outlast him.

**2025** — Net worth ~$150 billion. Berkshire Hathaway market cap ~$1 trillion. Still in the same Omaha house. Still driving to McDonald's.

## 七、关键人物关系时间轴

**Ben Graham** (1950–1976): Intellectual father; value investing foundation; never close personally
**Charlie Munger** (1959–2023): Business partner, intellectual conscience, closest friend; 64 years
**Carol Loomis** (1977–2022): Letters editor; over 40 years of collaboration
**Tom Murphy** (1985+): Role model for media operator; Buffett called him the best manager he ever knew
**Bill Gates** (1991–): Friend, philanthropic partner; Giving Pledge co-founder
**Greg Abel** (2018–): Designated successor; will run Berkshire operations
