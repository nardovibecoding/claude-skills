## 一、Charlie Rose 访谈精华 (2022, age 91)

### 论工作与幸福
[一手] "I tap dance to work. I've never been happier. I've got the most interesting, to me, job in the world."

[一手] "I've always been happy in the work, but I've never been happier." — at age 91, after pandemic isolation

[一手] "What makes me happiest is what I'm doing. One, I know I'll win over time. Two, I like being trusted by people. They say, look, I've saved money. I don't know what to do with it. I trust you."

### 论每日工作流程
[一手] "I get up a few minutes before seven, so I can catch the seven o'clock news. I always turn on CNBC. I look at what prices are doing in Europe and Japan. I call Mark at 8, with the market opening at 8.30, and I tell him exactly what to do for the day. I probably won't change it during the day."

[一手] "It's usually a percentage of something. I will say buy or sell X percent of the trading volume, and I'll give him a list of things. Then he does it all day. And I don't need to check with him. He sometimes does billions of dollars worth of business."

[推断] The simplicity of his daily process — one call, clear instructions, then hands off — reflects his philosophy about systems over activity. No heroic trading desk; just clear thinking executed calmly.

### 论第一笔投资（1942，年龄11岁）
[一手] "In 1942, 80 years ago, I took $114.75 — every penny I'd saved. And I bought three shares of City Service preferred. I put my whole net worth in. Since March 11th, 1942, I have never had less than 80% of my money in American business."

[一手] "I've owned a piece of American business at least 80% at all times. I don't want to own anything else."

### 论天才与智商
[一手] "I'm a bright guy who's terribly interested in what he does. I've spent a lifetime doing it. I've surrounded myself with people that bring out the best in me. And you don't need to be a genius in what I do. That's the good thing about it."

[一手] "If I went into physics or a whole lot of other subjects, I'd be an also-ran. But I am in a game where you probably need 120 points of IQ. But 170 doesn't do any better than 120. It may do worse."

### 论买企业 vs 买股票
[一手] "From that point [after reading Ben Graham], I never bought another stock. I bought businesses that happened to be publicly traded. I became an owner of a business, and I did not care whether a stock went up or down the next day or the next week or the next month or the next year."

[一手] "I'm not buying stocks. I'm buying pieces of overwhelmingly American business. I'm happy when stocks are going down. I'm happier when stocks are going down because I can buy more of them with the same amount of money. I'd be happy if I was a farmer. I'd want farmland to go down so I could buy more acreage."

### 论竞争本能
[一手] "I've got a competitive spirit, but you've got to be careful about what you compete in. I try to keep my competitive spirit in a game where I can win."

[一手] "I wouldn't call it a killer instinct. But when I want to do something, I always want to do it big." — on investment concentration

### 论家庭与人生决策
[一手] "The actual big acts in life are when you propose to your spouse. If you made the right decision and you get the right answer, you're 90% of the way home in life."

[一手] "I've lived in the same house since 1958. Something like 63 or 64 years. A quarter of the life of the country. I like being five minutes from the office. Just think if I had a 35-minute commute — let's say I save 30 minutes every day. Over 60 years, that's something."

## 二、Berkshire AGM Q&A 精华（Buffett & Munger）

### 论投资的简单本质
[一手] "Investing is not that complicated. I mean, the basic framework for it is simple. You didn't have to have a high IQ. You didn't have to have lots of investment smarts to buy junk bonds in 2002. You really just had to have sort of the courage of your convictions."

[一手] "The lesson of following logic rather than emotion is something that's obvious. And some people have great trouble with it, and others have less trouble."

### 论决策速度
[一手] "The truth is, if we can't make a decision in five minutes, we can't make it in five months. We're not going to learn enough in the following five months to make up for the fact that we went in deficient in the first place."

[一手] "So we make decisions — we can make a decision in five minutes very easily. I mean, it just is not that complicated."

### 论市场预测的无用性
[一手] "I've not earned any stars for my past economic predictions. And the good thing about my economic predictions, if I even do make them, is that I pay no attention to them myself."

[一手] "I can't recall a time when Charlie and I have looked at a business, either buying it in its entirety or buying pieces of it through the stock market, where macro conclusions ever enter into the discussion."

[一手] "You cannot get rich with a weather vane." — on following market predictions

### 论 Wall Street 分析师与经济学家
[一手] "If I said I was going to offer a bunch of predictions today, we would have a million people here. They're dying to have predictions. That's what a whole industry is built upon." — on the uselessness of forecasters

[Munger 一手] "All of these economists with 160 IQs that spend their life studying it — can you name me one super wealthy economist that's ever made money out of securities?"

[Munger 一手] "The king used to hire the magician or the forecaster, and he'd look in sheep guts for an answer as to how to handle the next war. There's a lot of that still going on." — on Wall Street forecasting

### 论风险管理
[一手] "We think the best way to minimize risk is to think." — on asset allocation models

[一手] "We are never going to risk what we have and need for what we don't have and don't need. We'll still find things to do where we can make money, but we don't have to stretch to do it."

[一手] "We not only want to sleep well on those nights [crises], we want to be thinking about things to do with some excess money we might have around."

### 论犯错与遗漏
[一手] "The mistakes we made are of two kinds. One is when we didn't invest at all in something we understood that was cheap. The second was starting in on something that could have been a very large investment and not maximizing it."

[一手] "I set out to buy 100 million shares of Walmart pre-split at about 23. We bought a little, and then it moved up a little bit. And I thought, well, maybe it'll come back... That thumb-sucking, reluctance to pay a little more, the current cost is in the area of 10 billion." — on the Walmart miss

[Munger 一手] "At least we are constantly thinking about the past occasions when we blew opportunities. Since those don't hit financial reports, most people don't bother thinking about them. At least that is a mistake we don't make."

### 论宏观经济与历史
[一手] "Everything that happens in the investment world reminds me of something or other. I mean, every day. If it's a boring day, it reminds me of something."

[一手] "What we learn from history is that people don't learn from history. And you certainly see that in financial markets all the time."

### 论如果重新开始（小资金）
[一手] "I think if I'd been running a partnership a couple of years ago with a small amount of money, I think I'd have probably been 100 percent in Korea [where stocks were extremely cheap]. I would be looking around for something that was very mispriced and that I understood."

[一手] "You do not have to have tons of good ideas in this business. You just have a good idea that's worth a ton occasionally."

## 三、论信任与声誉 (2008金融危机背景)

### 高盛注资 (2008)
[一手] "I said, we'll give you $10 billion under these circumstances. But we'll do it. We'll do it tomorrow." — on the Goldman Sachs deal at the height of the 2008 crisis

[一手] "They had to have somebody who counted besides people whose position it was to lie." — on why Goldman needed Berkshire's credibility, not just its cash

### 论信誉的积累
[一手] "I started on March 11, 1942. And I had a father that was the right father. I was lucky."

[推断] Buffett frames the Goldman moment not as a calculated move but as the payoff of 66 years of consistent behavior. Reputation is the compounding asset he values most.

## 四、论生活哲学（散见各采访）

[一手] "I measure success by how many people you want to love you actually do love you. Money is a by-product of doing what you love."

[一手] "I enjoy two things about it. One, I know I'll win over time. Two, I like being trusted by people." — on what makes him happy

[一手] "The bottom 3% and for sure the top 1% in the United States all live better than John D. Rockefeller was living when I was six years old. John D. Rockefeller was the richest man in the world." — on American material progress

[一手] "There are decades when nothing happens. And then there are weeks when decades happen." — quoting Lenin; on the nature of crises and opportunities
